Tran3D: from Zero to One

Last year, I have been working on a startup product—Tran3D. In this article, I would like to share with you some of my designs from strategy to interface.

OVERVIEW

About the project

Tran3D is an online 3D printing service platform where distributed 3D printing resources are gathered, shared, and applied.

How it works

Tran3D runs a C2C business model. It facilitates transactions between 3D printer owners and people who want to make 3D prints. Service bureaus can join the platform to offer 3D printing services while customers can select one of the best solutions we recommend to get their customized models printed.

My roles

Being one of the co-founders, I led the UI/UX design and front-end development from start to finish.

As a designer in particular, I worked on

  • User research
  • Product design
  • Sketches, wireframes and mock-ups
  • Visual and interaction design

How I started

Working closely with my partners, I first made a Q&A sheet that depicted our potential users.

User Brief

Who are the target audiences?

Modern people who enjoy customizing things or transforming ideas into reality using technology.

What are the audience types and their differences?

There are three main types. 1. Common individuals—people who need 3D printed products, but without 3D printers; 2. Designers—people who are capable of producing high-quality 3D models; 3. Manufacturers—people who own 3D printers, can either be individuals or companies.

Where can audiences be found?

Most likely, they can be found in universities or manufactural industry. They might also have active accounts in 3D print forums.

What personality does Tran3D want to project to the audience?

Simple, rapid, technological, playful and high quality.

Based on the user brief, I interviewed 50 people via telephone and finally planned out our design goals.

  • To facilitate an intuitive and seamless experience when it comes to customizing 3D printing products
  • To design a personable and engaging user interface
  • To create convenient and powerful functionalities that can manage services and transactions

Next, I will choose three representatives that best accomplished our design goals. And I will talk about their design processes and implementation results.

START A 3D PRINT

The Problem

3D printing features rapid prototyping and product customization. It enables consumer products to be made on­-demand, eliminating the restriction to craftsmanship and manufacture, as well as reducing the waste from overproduction and overpricing.

On the consumer side, however their expertise, most people expressed their interests about transforming their ideas into reality and their curiosity about 3D printed objects during the interviews.

So here comes the problem—how might we get customers onboard in order to fully deliver the quality of 3D printing to them?

The Analysis

To introduce complex technology home, Tran3D must provide customers with an easy and fast channel to getting their hands on real 3D printed products. The main idea is to build a module that helps users start a 3D print and guides them quickly through a customization workflow before they reach shopping cart. Some core design principles were identified:

  • Prominent entries—CTAs draw attentions and trigger interests.
  • Easy start—Tasks that are easier to do are a better starting point for people with low motivation (refer to BJ Fogg Behavior Model).
  • Limited choices—It’s often easier to make a decision when presented with only few options.
  • Hidden expert-level interfaces and terminologies—Expert users are the minority.

Overall, the customization workflow of 3D print should be lucid, easy, efficient, and interesting.

The Exploring

I started my exploration by scoping the user flow into three phases: upload my models, select a material category, and choose a 3D printing service. In this way, a complex and tedious process was effectively decomposed into parts whose tasks were easier to understand and goals were clearer to achieve.

I then played with a few key design concepts while creating mid-fidelity wireframes. Those concepts were:

  • Create visually striking CTAs that compel users to click.
  • Start from the task that is easiest and most requisite.
  • Unlock next phase until the current one is completed, bringing fewer choices to users.
  • Prioritize imperative parameters, use straightforward copies and hide expert-level content.
  • Integrate all three steps in one page for an unbreakable experience. This integrity keeps data consistent and interaction coherent.

The Highlight

Starting a 3D print is a highly-focused task for users. The primary goal is to find the most suitable 3D printing service to have uploaded models well-printed. Due to 3D printing's nature, however, the task requires deep focus, manual input and selection, and back-and-forth decision makings from users.

The innovation I made was utilizing SPA (Single Page Application), where users could accomplish the task all in one page. During usability testing, I noticed better results in terms of effectiveness and efficiency, causing less drop-off in this page.

The Result

After unifying back-end data with team, I finalized the flow as indicated below.

It creates a clear sense of assistance and reduces the unnecessity to minimum so that non-expert users can understand how things work without any direct explanation. Useful images and friendly language are added throughout. It also has animations and transitions, which adds a bit of fun. I am optimistic that users will get onboard and progress through all three phases with little friction, leading to successful customizations of their 3D prints.

DISCOVER THE USER PROFILE

The Problem

Tran3D in essence is a 3D printing community where users design, prototype, sell and buy custom products and services. However, different users have different roles. Given a user,

  • How can we distinguish his roles from others'?
  • Which functions should we assign to him?
  • How can we help showcase his characteristics and develop his own "brand"?

The Analysis

Upon discovering a user, one has to identify that person's roles and characteristics intuitively and efficiently.

Designers productively transfer their concepts into printable designs. Manufacturers register 3D printers and promote their products. And like the rest of individuals, both can follow, collect or purchase what they are interested in. According to this, I listed some keywords to guide my design.

  • Architecture—Interpreting underlying data structure and class inheritance reduces confliction between design and development.
  • Hierarchy—The item that first grabs the eye’s attention is at the top of the hierarchy. Each item that draws attention next is subordinate to the one before it.
  • Coloring—One of the easiest ways to distinguish objects is through colors.
  • Personalization—Users get motivated if they are provided with more personalized choices.

The Exploration

After sketching out several candidates, I made the following design decisions.

1. Information Architecture

I separated users into three categories: common individuals, designers and manufacturers. All types shared a same template—Basic information was listed out; designs, collections, orders, followings and followers were organized into tabs. It is worth noting that designers were given an extra section to introduce their backgrounds, concepts and portfolios while manufacturers were given a shop page and a dashboard to manage printers, orders and etc.

2. Visual Hierarchy

I used Reversed-ソ Pattern—Our eye starts scanning from top left, then diagonally down, stopping at the bottom right. Within a short break, information on the top right might draw some attention. The diagonal line guides us to read the main content and the dot serves as a little weight for equilibrium. For example, the reading flow of user profile starts with avatar and username. It is followed by content categories, and a list of specific items. Finally, some user statistics that are less important are located on the top right. (A special thank to Jeff Zhu for correction.)

3. Color Scheme

I chose the color palettes similar to flat design. Complementary colors made personality bold and stand out. Analogous colors kept our page clean and calm.

4. Personalization

Besides user settings, I highlighted 'Register', 'Create' and 'Follow' buttons and encouraged users to enrich their profiles. Also, behaviors such as creating and collecting would be included in followers' home feed, which kept the character active in social network.

MANAGE SHOP SERVICES

The Problem

Tran3D schedules 3D printing services based on data and facts. It is important for manufacturers to provide authentic and intriguing data. This is non-trivial work:

  1. Data encompasses various facets of 3D printing—It includes printers, materials, colors and layer thicknesses, to name a few.
  2. Pricing strategies are extremely complex—The strategies vary from case to case and they are hard to get translated from mathematical models.

Therefore how can I help them upload the data in an intuitive way and how can I organize the data for a clearer reference?

The Analysis

To get better insights, I interviewed 8 people who own 3D printers. Based on their thoughts about products and marketing, I made three personas.

Manufacturer Persona

Frank

Frank, aged 32, is an assistant professor of material science at university. His lab owns 5 industrial 3D printers which could support more than 40 printable materials. Frank is very intelligent about 3D print technology and especially, he knows materials inside out. He is an expert in building mathematical models and experimenting new things.

Sarah

Sarah is a clerk at Tiertime, a 3D print company in Beijing. She works in service team that is responsible for a chain of 3D print services. Since she is not an expert, her job is to maintain or update data provided by her technical colleagues. In order to deal with different platforms, Sarah needs to quickly understand interfaces and exchanging information with the team.

David

David is a technology enthusiast and he is only 20. His father bought him a desktop-level 3D printer on his last birthday, and he wants to make the full use of it. David knows how to use the printer however, he has no idea about how to provide 3D print service—He can only create simple pricing strategies but he is not sure if they would work.

Their user needs can be described as:

  1. Hierarchical—It is easier to navigate and understand the system by organizing content hierarchically.
  2. Standardized—Interactions are using the same paradigm so that users can understand.
  3. Complete—More complete data increases a manufacturer's credibility, therefore popularity.
  4. Intuitive—Uploading data is simple, not learned.
  5. Flexible—The system allows manufacturers to manipulate, irrelevant to their backgrounds.
  6. Foreseeable—Any preview of the outcome would help manufacturers circumvent mistakes, lowering the possibility of failed strategies.
  7. Reversible—Data that is incorrect or outdated can be removed or modified.

The Exploration

During the design process, I tried to deliver the ideas above. Here, I will illustrate one by one:

1. Divide concepts into hierarchy.

A manufacturer may lack motivation to publish all service data within a single upload. So I divided the whole process and organized them into levels. From the shop to specific objects like printer and material, a hierarchical design would very much facilitate user flows.

2. Unify layouts and interactions.

I chose to use a page component that had consistently shown up in Tran3D—the modal. This was because I did not want to overwhelm the user with an unfamiliar UI. A modal also made manufacturers concentrate on what they were doing. Within a modal, data uploading was splitted into steps and there was a top navigation bar indicating the progress.

3. Include data that helps SEO and discoverability.

Titles, descriptions, tags, photos and categories are properties for manufacturers to better describe their services. These features can be descriptive and educational to someone outside of their niches. Complete data speaks aloud for the services.

4. Utilize shapes and colors.

Making a pricing strategy is one of the most challenging problems in respect of interaction design—Strategies in 3D print industry involve a variety of factors, not only the physical properties but also laboring influences.

A common mathematical model is linear polynomial which sums up a number of terms. The quantity of terms depends and terms are flexible since they have different interpretations.

When I tried to illustrate this polynomial using simple shapes and colors, I found that building a one-to-one correspondence between a term and a color was the most intuitive solution. It turned a complex math formula into objects that can be easily cognized by human beings.

5. Add flexibility to the system.

The module must be adaptive to users with different backgrounds. Consider a user with non-expert background. He might need notes for terminologies, tips for interactions and default values for complicated data. On the contrary, an expert might need more specifications and details. And he wants to enable more terms in the polynomial to better control his pricing strategy.

6. Preview simulated results.

Manufacturers will get a live preview of what the price would be after proposing a pricing strategy. The result is calculated by applying the pricing strategy to a test case we provided. It even gives an instant feedback as the user make any adjustments to the linear polynomial. This functionality helps manufacturers find a suitable pricing strategy. It also makes them feel powerful so that they can trust our system.

7. Provide CRUD operations.

Besides basic creating and reading, service data like printers, materials and even pricing strategies are revisable if one made any mistakes.

The Result

From the shop's public page to a modal that creates material and pricing strategies, I am satisfied that my design goals are accomplished across different pages. The design is simple yet powerful, not only providing intuitive interfaces but also tailoring to different user needs. Meanwhile, a lot of common elements are reused, contributing to the system consistency.

CONCLUSION AND REFLECTION

At the early stage of a startup company, I wanted to deliver a minimum viable product and provide a flexible structure that could scale naturally as the website grows. Looking back, the project has included all necessary functionalities and built upon a scalable framework. It has successfully leveraged the design language to promote a high-end technology, decoding the secret of 3D printing into context that people can comprehend and make use of.

As a designer and a developer, the project also made me appreciate the importance of coordinating different perspectives and collaborating with different roles. It became one of the most precious experiences in my career.
