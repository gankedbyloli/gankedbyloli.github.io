Establishing a New Business Tool Suite

In 2018, Booking.com, one of the largest travel e-commerce company unprecedentedly expanded its investment in China. In order to adapt to this unique and ever-evolving market, China team quickly strategized rich marketing and operation plans for the purpose of customer acquisition and retention.

TOOL SUITE

Background

At the beginning, quick solutions including marketing landings, operation campaigns, SMS and push notifications were created to address immediate business needs, aiming for more traffic as well as more engagement.

General Problem

However, after piloting a few marketing and operation programs, results were less than satisfactory. Three critical issues were discovered from the product and design side.

  • No infrastructure—there were no standardized workflow or tools to enable that; few services, features or design elements were reusable
  • Extremely low productivity—technical dependencies were everywhere, communication cost was high and human resources were lacked
  • Disconnection between product and business operation—few designers were involved and those early programs did not carried the same personality as our apps and sites

High-level Solution

As the only product/UX designer on the team, I suggested we build a platform incorporating a matrix of tools, with each of them serving to cater one major need from business. Similar to the system of differentiation, as defined in Gartner's pace-layered application strategy, this platform on the one hand, communicates with core algorithms and databases, on the other hand, provides various end-points so that it is adaptive and agile enough to support different service calls and frequent front-end changes.

Objectives of the tool matrix were:

  • Facilitate an effective and sustainable workflow across teams; provide reusable services, features or design elements
  • Decouple dependencies, reduce communication cost and free up human resources
  • Set up design direction as it generates pattern library to align business with product

I will choose one representative tool to best showcase what the problems were and how we solved them.

CMS - CAMPAIGN PAGE

Problem

Campaign pages are extremely common in Chinese e-commerce platforms. One of their distinguishing characteristics is that they are content-focused. Content, though taking various forms, is usually created, grouped and organized based on business impact or customer interest. Some examples are listed.

By working closely with the operation team, we understood our internal needs were:

  • Can rapidly create, group and organize unique content for each campaign
  • Can flexibly change and deploy content as needed
  • Can follow certain copy and design guidance to ensure quality

On the other hand, our customer needs were:

  • Can browse and navigate through the content easily
  • Can very well understand the main theme that the content reflects
  • Can build up interest by viewing properties, incentives or travel-related articles

Thus, our goals can be inferred—to achieve self-service campaign creation, implementation and modification and to strike balance between weight, quality and entropy of the content at the meantime.

This yields for a Content Management System.

1. Structure and Hierarchy

The first step I took was understanding structure and hierarchy of the content. I would like to identify and extract patterns that can be generalized into tools and processes.

Since our main approach is listing featured properties, incentives, or articles, I categorized all campaigns into two: commodity-focused and inspiration-focused. The former exploits business opportunity, incentivizing customers to book immediately while the latter is seen as an inspirational source to trigger customer interest before any travel plan.

I created mind mappings—for each category, different page elements were generalized and extracted. Each of them fell into a specific hierarchy, whether it was a template, a pattern, a component, or an atom.

The results were highly correlated with the final information architecture of the CMS. Additionally, I included items that were optional or foreseeable in the future. Creating placeholders for them would make the CMS robust and scalable in the long run.

2. Generation Flow

The content generation flow was essentially an inverse of how I decomposed it. The concept is similar to Atomic Design in which everything begins with the smallest element of the interface: the atom, then forms molecule, organism, template and eventually the page.

Our operation team would follow similar steps to generate content: create a blank page, choose a template, choose a template create modular sections, choose a template add components, and finally choose a template populate data, copy and visual elements.

3. Design Values and Guideline

Although campaign content is highly diversified and flexible, it should not drift away from the personality that our products have already had. In this case, we wanted to set a design direction that all generated content can automatically follow.

Extensive research was conducted to figure out opportunities that lay within brand identity, products and customer expectations.

Highlighted Research Questions

What is the main message our brand tries to deliver?

Our brand encourages everyone to travel boldly using the reliable services we provide.

What is the common mental model in which our loyalty customers think of us?

In focus group, we found out 1) pros: Booking.com had various properties, trusted reviews and easy booking processes 2) cons: there were few incentives and little inspiration they could get.

What are the design values we need to keep anyway?

We design for people. Our job is to make it as simple and effective as possible for everyone involved to succeed when they interact with our service.

What are the aspects that a campaign page can differ from the products?

We want to get our customers attracted and inspired. We would like to introduce an interesting and enjoyable atmosphere.

While comparing campaign pages to our general products, we found generality in terms of clarity and effectiveness, yet peculiarity in terms of intriguing and delightful experience. Therefore we decided to use clarity and interestingness as our design values for campaign pages.

The next step was to translate them into something tangible that could be the foundation of every campaign. Here, I want to highlight three basics that push our values forward. They are rounded shapes, white space and subtle gradients.

For example, I followed the basics when designing variants of property cards.

Variants were specific components. I then transformed a group of similar components into a generic one that was reusable. I also dissected the generic component and rethought about more of its variations by adding or removing an element of it.

In this way, we gradually establish and enrich the design pattern library.

Mock-ups

For the content management tool itself, we used Ant Design as its design language, providing clear information and navigation for the internal users. Below are some mock-ups.

Our internal users would first initiate a new campaign and select its campaign category: either commodity-focused or inspiration-focused.

The tool then led them to set up the new page properly including SEO information, section modules and navigations.

Section modules, in particular, were categorized according to the mind mappings previously.

Users would create any section and populate components inside. For example, they could create a section containing a variety of regular properties. They would decide how to group or which elements need to be surfaced. Even though it was quite flexible to generate the content, users could still manage to ensure a clear and consistent UI style with our products.

After launching the new tool, we saw significant performance increase from the campaign pages CMS generated. In terms of productivity, we saved 20 people day on average per each campaign. In terms of quality, the amount of interactions happened inside a campaign page also increased by 35%.
